Job Search and Unemployment

Consider an unemployed worker who each period can draw two independently and identically distributed wage offers from the cumulative probability distribution function \(F(w)\).

The worker will work forever at the same wage after having once accepted an offer. In the event of unemployment during a period, the worker receives unemployment compensation \(c\).

The worker derives a decision rule to maximize \(E\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\beta^{t}y_t\), where \(y_t=w\) or \(y_t=c\), depending on whether she is employed or unemployed. \(\beta\) is the discounting factor.

Let \(V(w)\) be the value of \(E\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\beta^{t}y_t\) for a currently unemployed worker who has best offer \(w\) in hand.

Theoretical basics

Exercise 1

Formulate the Bellman equation to the workerâs problem. Which of the two wages currently offered in the period will the worker take once he accepts a job?

\[ \begin{aligned} V(w) &= max \{ \frac{w}{1-\beta}, c + \beta E V(w^\ast) \}\\ & = max\{ \frac{w}{1-\beta}, c+ \beta\int V(w^\ast)dF(w^\ast)\} \end{aligned} \] The first term on the right hand side of the Bellman equation is the value when the worker accepts the job offer where \(w = max\{ w_1, w_2\}\). \(\frac{w}{1-\beta}\) is the result of the infinite geometric sum \(\sum_{t=0}^\infty \beta^t w\). If the worker decides to accept a job offer she will always decide in favor of the higher of the two offers since it maximizes future returns. The second term on the right hand side of the equation denotes the case where the worker declines both job offers and receives unemployment compensation plus the discounted expected value of future earnings with new wage offer \(w^\ast\). The expectations can be written as an integral over the probability distribution of the wage offers.

# Value function
get_value <- function(accept,
                      V_obj,
                      wage_offer,
                      compensation,
                      beta,
                      w_grid,
                      var,
                      mu){
  if(accept){
    return(wage_offer/(1-beta))
  }
  # The expected value of V(w) is computed via Monte Carlo integration
  else{
    set.seed(1234)

The value function is constant until the reservation wage has been reached. The reservation wage is the lowest wage the worker is willing to accept an offer and work. Below the reservation wage the expected value of future earnings is too low and the worker remains unemployed and waits for a better offer. For all wages above the reservation wage the value function increases in \(w\) since then the lifetime value is given by \(\frac{w}{1-\beta}\). The kink in the function denotes the reservation wage.

Choosing a large \(\epsilon\) allows to explore more state action pairs more often. However, a large \(\epsilon\) is far from being optimal if used all the time. An \(\epsilon\) too small, however, does not ensure that we explore enough and greedy actions might not always be the optimal thing to do. So starting with a large \(\epsilon\) ensures that we are exploring the state action space and the in i decreasing epsilon ensures that our policy becomes more optimal. A high \(\alpha\) at the beginning ensures that the worker learns relatively fast and overwrites old information relatively quickly. If \(\alpha\) decreases in i, the agent learns less quickly, however, she considers old information in more depth.
